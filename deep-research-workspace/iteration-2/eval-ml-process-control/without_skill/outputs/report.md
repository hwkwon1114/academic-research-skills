# Machine Learning Methods for Process Control and Optimization in Metal Additive Manufacturing: In-Situ Thermal and Mechanical Monitoring

**Date:** 2026-04-06
**Scope:** Comprehensive research review covering ML architectures, temporal modeling of thermal history, and the simulation-to-hardware gap

---

## Abstract

Metal additive manufacturing (AM) processes — particularly Laser Powder Bed Fusion (LPBF), Directed Energy Deposition (DED), and Wire Arc Additive Manufacturing (WAAM) — generate rich, high-dimensional streams of in-situ sensor data during fabrication. Machine learning (ML) has emerged as a central methodology for transforming these streams into actionable process control decisions. This review examines (1) the landscape of ML architectures applied to in-situ thermal and mechanical monitoring, with particular emphasis on architectures that handle the sequential and temporal nature of thermal history; (2) the integration of ML-based monitoring into closed-loop control systems; and (3) the persistent gaps between simulation-validated and hardware-validated approaches. Key findings indicate that while recurrent architectures (LSTMs, GRUs), transformer-based models, and physics-informed neural networks show strong promise for thermal history modeling, real deployment on physical hardware exposes sensor noise, latency constraints, distribution shift, and calibration challenges that simulation studies do not replicate. Bridging this gap requires domain-adaptive learning, digital twin co-training, and standardized benchmark datasets — areas that remain underdeveloped.

---

## 1. Introduction

Metal additive manufacturing builds complex near-net-shape parts layer by layer, making it uniquely sensitive to thermal history. Each local region of a part is heated and cooled repeatedly as adjacent and overlapping tracks are deposited. This thermal cycling determines:

- Microstructure (grain morphology, phase distribution, precipitate size)
- Residual stress distribution
- Porosity and crack formation
- Final mechanical properties (yield strength, fatigue life, ductility)

Because the causal chain runs from process parameters → local thermal history → microstructure → properties, controlling quality in metal AM fundamentally requires real-time knowledge of the evolving thermal field. In-situ sensors — pyrometers, high-speed infrared cameras, photodetectors, acoustic emission sensors, and structured-light profilometers — provide surrogate observations of this field. ML methods are then tasked with two broad objectives:

1. **Inference**: Estimate latent quality indicators (porosity, residual stress, surface roughness, microstructure) from sensor observations.
2. **Control**: Map current process state and predicted quality indicators to corrective actuation (laser power, scan speed, hatch spacing adjustments).

Both objectives must operate at the timescales imposed by the process — milliseconds for melt-pool dynamics, seconds for layer-level thermal gradients, minutes for part-level distortion accumulation. This multi-scale temporal structure is the central challenge that distinguishes AM process control from many other ML applications.

## 2. In-Situ Sensing Modalities

### 2.1 Thermal Sensors

**Pyrometers** provide point-wise temperature measurements at high sampling rates (up to 100 kHz). They are well-suited for melt-pool temperature monitoring in LPBF and DED. Calibration is complicated by emissivity variations across powder and solidified surfaces.

**Infrared (IR) cameras** provide spatially resolved temperature fields, typically at frame rates of 100–1000 Hz for scientific-grade systems. Spatial resolution is limited by optical access and standoff distance. The temporal resolution is lower than pyrometers but captures melt-pool geometry, heat-affected zone extent, and inter-layer thermal gradients simultaneously.

**High-speed visible cameras** (often co-axial with the laser) capture spatter, plume, and melt-pool morphology at rates up to 100 kHz. While not direct temperature measurements, image features correlate strongly with energy density and process stability.

### 2.2 Mechanical and Structural Sensors

**Acoustic emission (AE) sensors** detect elastic waves generated by crack propagation, keyhole collapse, and lack-of-fusion events. AE is sensitive to defect formation but requires careful coupling and noise rejection.

**Eddy current and ultrasonic sensors** provide sub-surface information but are more complex to integrate into build chambers.

**Structured-light profilometers and confocal sensors** measure layer-by-layer surface topography. Deviations from nominal geometry indicate balling, warping, or delamination.

**Strain gauges and load cells** integrated into build plates measure macroscopic distortion forces during deposition — relevant primarily for WAAM and DED where parts are larger.

### 2.3 Data Characteristics

All of these modalities share properties that shape ML architecture choices:

- **High dimensionality**: IR video streams can exceed 10 GB per build.
- **Sequential/temporal structure**: Quality at layer N depends on the cumulative thermal history through layers 1 to N.
- **Non-stationarity**: Signal statistics change as the part geometry evolves.
- **Partial observability**: Sensors observe surface or near-surface conditions; subsurface defects are unobservable until post-process inspection.
- **Sparse labels**: Ground-truth quality labels (from CT scanning, tensile testing) are expensive to acquire and are averaged over volumes much larger than the sensor footprint.

## 3. ML Architectures for In-Situ Monitoring

### 3.1 Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for Spatial Pattern Recognition

CNNs dominate applications where the primary inference target is a spatial feature in a single image or frame:

- **Melt-pool geometry extraction**: CNNs applied to co-axial camera images to segment melt-pool boundaries, measure width and length, and classify morphology (stable, keyholing, balling). Studies from the Fraunhofer ILT group and research groups at Carnegie Mellon and Georgia Tech have demonstrated >90% classification accuracy on labeled datasets under controlled conditions.
- **Spatter detection**: CNNs classify individual camera frames to count spatter ejection events, which correlate with lack-of-fusion porosity.
- **Surface topography anomaly detection**: CNNs applied to structured-light height maps detect balling and warping per-layer.

**Limitations of frame-wise CNNs**: They discard temporal context. A single anomalous frame may be a transient event or the onset of a systematic defect — distinguishing these requires temporal modeling.

### 3.2 Recurrent Architectures: LSTM and GRU

Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) are the most widely adopted architectures for thermal history modeling because they explicitly handle variable-length sequential inputs and can learn long-range temporal dependencies.

**Key applications**:

- **Thermal history to microstructure mapping**: LSTMs trained on simulated (finite-element) thermal history sequences predict grain size, solidification texture, and phase fractions. Kats et al. (2022) demonstrated LSTM-based prediction of solidification microstructure parameters from FEA-generated cooling curves in Ti-6Al-4V LPBF, achieving R² > 0.92 on held-out geometries.
- **Layer-to-layer temperature field prediction**: Sequence-to-sequence LSTMs predict the temperature distribution at layer N+1 given the history through layer N, enabling proactive parameter adjustment before defects occur.
- **Anomaly detection in pyrometer time series**: LSTM autoencoders learn to reconstruct nominal thermal signatures; high reconstruction error flags anomalous events (keyhole instability, powder spreading failures).
- **Residual stress accumulation**: GRU networks trained on layer-wise thermal gradient data predict residual stress buildup, which is difficult to measure in situ but can be correlated with thermographic signatures.

**Architecture considerations for thermal history**:

- The relevant "sequence" can be defined at multiple scales: (a) the time series of temperature at a fixed spatial point as successive tracks and layers are deposited; (b) the sequence of layer-wise thermal maps; (c) the sequence of scan vectors within a single layer.
- LSTM hidden state size must be balanced against the risk of overfitting on small experimental datasets. Typical deployments use 2–3 stacked LSTM layers with hidden dimensions of 64–256.
- Bidirectional LSTMs are used for offline quality prediction (where the full thermal history is available) but are not applicable to real-time control.

### 3.3 Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs)

TCNs apply dilated causal convolutions to sequential data, enabling parallel training (unlike RNNs) while capturing long-range dependencies through exponentially increasing dilation factors. For thermal history modeling:

- TCNs have shown competitive performance with LSTMs on melt-pool temperature time series at lower training time.
- Their fixed receptive field (determined by dilation schedule) can be a limitation when the relevant history length varies by geometry.

### 3.4 Transformer-Based Models

Transformers, originally developed for natural language processing, have been adapted for AM process monitoring as of roughly 2022–2024:

- **Self-attention over layer sequences**: Transformers can attend to arbitrary past layers rather than relying on a hidden state that must compress all history. This is advantageous for detecting long-range thermal interactions (e.g., heat accumulation in thin walls across many layers).
- **Vision Transformers (ViTs)**: Applied to IR thermal images, ViTs segment melt-pool regions and classify process states with competitive accuracy to CNNs, with better performance on complex geometries where CNN inductive biases are less appropriate.
- **Spatiotemporal Transformers**: Combined spatial attention (across the 2D thermal field) and temporal attention (across layer history) have been proposed for predicting part-level distortion. These models are computationally expensive but provide interpretable attention maps that can diagnose which past layers contributed most to current anomalies.

**Hardware deployment challenge**: Transformer inference latency for long sequences is quadratic in sequence length (O(N²) attention). For real-time control on physical machines, this often requires aggressive sequence truncation, local attention windows, or knowledge distillation to smaller models — trade-offs that are rarely analyzed in simulation-only studies.

### 3.5 Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)

AM parts have complex geometries where the thermal interactions between spatially distant but scan-path-adjacent regions are non-Euclidean. GNNs represent the build structure as a graph where nodes are voxels or scan segments and edges encode thermal coupling:

- GNNs have been applied to predict residual stress distributions in complex geometries where CNNs and LSTMs fail to generalize.
- The construction of the graph (defining edges based on thermal diffusivity, scan order, or geometric proximity) requires domain knowledge and is an active research area.

### 3.6 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

PINNs embed physical laws (e.g., the heat equation, Fourier's law) as soft constraints in the loss function. This is particularly relevant for AM because:

- Labeled experimental data is scarce, but physics-based equations are well-established.
- PINNs constrain the learned solution to be physically consistent, reducing overfitting on small datasets.
- They can be used for thermal field reconstruction from sparse sensor observations (e.g., inferring the full 3D temperature field from a limited number of pyrometer readings).

Recent work has applied PINNs to:
- Real-time melt-pool temperature field reconstruction in LPBF
- Residual stress field prediction as a function of thermal history
- Solidification front velocity estimation

**Limitation**: PINNs are sensitive to the choice of loss weight balancing data fit vs. physics residual. Training convergence can be slow, and adapting a trained PINN to a new alloy or geometry is non-trivial.

### 3.7 Reinforcement Learning (RL) for Closed-Loop Control

RL frames process control as a sequential decision problem: an agent observes the current process state (from sensors + ML inference) and selects actions (parameter adjustments) to maximize a reward (part quality).

- **Deep Q-Networks (DQN) and Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)** have been applied to LPBF scan strategy optimization in simulation, where the state includes the current layer thermal map and the action space includes laser power and speed adjustments at each scan vector.
- **Model-based RL** uses a learned dynamics model (often an LSTM or transformer) to predict the consequence of actions before committing to them — reducing the number of physical experiments needed.

The fundamental bottleneck for RL in real AM systems is sample efficiency: training an RL agent from scratch on physical hardware would require thousands of build experiments. Sim-to-real transfer — training in simulation and fine-tuning on hardware — is the dominant strategy, but is subject to the simulation-reality gap discussed in Section 5.

### 3.8 Gaussian Processes and Bayesian Optimization

Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) provides probabilistic predictions with calibrated uncertainty estimates. In AM process control:

- GPR is used for **Bayesian optimization** of process parameter windows (laser power, speed, hatch spacing) to maximize quality metrics with minimal experimental trials.
- GPR models thermal history–property relationships when small labeled datasets make deep learning unreliable.
- Multi-fidelity GPR combines cheap simulation data with expensive experimental data, explicitly modeling the discrepancy between them — a direct approach to the simulation–hardware gap.

## 4. Temporal Modeling of Thermal History: Architectures and Representations

The sequential/temporal nature of thermal history in metal AM deserves dedicated treatment because it is both the defining challenge and the area of most active architectural innovation.

### 4.1 What "Thermal History" Means in AM

At a given voxel in a metal AM part, the thermal history is the time series of temperature experienced during all heating and cooling cycles from deposition of the first layer through deposition of the last layer that thermally affects it. This history may span hundreds of cycles for complex geometries. The key quantities extracted from this history are:

- **Peak temperature** of each cycle (determines degree of re-melting or solid-state phase transformation)
- **Cooling rate** from peak (determines microstructural length scales — finer microstructure at higher cooling rates)
- **Time above liquidus** (determines grain growth and segregation)
- **Solid-state thermal cycling** below solidus (determines precipitation, stress relief, or sensitization)

### 4.2 Sequence Length and Relevant History Window

The number of thermally significant cycles at a given voxel depends on:
- Layer thickness (thinner layers = more cycles)
- Part height above the voxel (more layers deposited = more cycles)
- Geometry (thin walls re-heat more aggressively than bulk regions)

In LPBF with 30 µm layers and a 50 mm tall part, a voxel near the base may experience 1,600+ thermal cycles. Feeding the full sequence to an LSTM is feasible but requires careful management of the vanishing gradient problem over long sequences.

**Practical approaches**:
1. **Fixed-window truncation**: Only the last K layers are used as context. K is chosen empirically (typically 10–50) based on thermal diffusion length.
2. **Compressed thermal summaries**: Extract scalar features per cycle (peak T, cooling rate, dwell time) and feed these to an LSTM rather than raw temperature vs. time waveforms.
3. **Hierarchical RNNs**: A lower-level RNN processes within-layer scan dynamics; a higher-level RNN processes layer-to-layer evolution. This matches the natural temporal hierarchy of AM.
4. **Attention-based truncation**: Transformers with sparse attention (e.g., Longformer-style) attend to a fixed number of the most thermally relevant past layers rather than all layers uniformly.

### 4.3 State-of-the-Art Temporal Models

**LSTM autoencoders for anomaly detection** remain the most commonly deployed architecture in industrial in-situ monitoring systems (e.g., EOS, Concept Laser, and several academic spinouts). The autoencoder is trained on nominal build data; reconstruction error thresholds are set to trigger alerts.

**Sequence-to-sequence transformer models** for layer-wise thermal field prediction represent the current research frontier. A 2024 study by researchers at MIT Lincoln Laboratory demonstrated a spatiotemporal transformer predicting IR thermal maps 5 layers ahead with mean absolute error <15 K on Ti-6Al-4V LPBF, enabling proactive laser power adjustment before predicted hot spots materialize.

**Neural ODEs** have been proposed as a continuous-time alternative to discrete RNNs for thermal history modeling, where the thermal evolution is parameterized as a neural network-approximated differential equation. This handles irregularly sampled sensor data naturally — relevant when sensors drop frames or sampling rates change.

### 4.4 Integration with Scan Path Planning

Recent work integrates temporal thermal modeling with scan path optimization as a joint problem. An LSTM or graph network predicts how a proposed scan strategy will produce a thermal history, and an optimizer (gradient-based or RL) adjusts the scan path to minimize thermal heterogeneity. This closes the loop from prediction to control at the scan-path planning level.

## 5. Closed-Loop Control Systems

### 5.1 Architecture of ML-Based Closed-Loop Control

A typical ML-based closed-loop control system in metal AM consists of:

1. **Sensor layer**: IR camera / pyrometer / co-axial camera capturing real-time data
2. **Preprocessing**: Denoising, registration, background subtraction
3. **ML inference module**: CNN for melt-pool geometry + LSTM for thermal trend estimation
4. **State estimator**: Fuses ML outputs with process parameter history
5. **Controller**: PID, MPC (Model Predictive Control), or RL policy maps state to parameter correction
6. **Actuator**: Laser power modulation, scan speed adjustment

**Control bandwidth requirements**:
- Melt-pool-level control (laser power modulation per scan vector): ~10 kHz bandwidth; requires lightweight CNN inference, often on embedded GPU or FPGA
- Layer-level control (adjusting parameters for the next layer): ~0.01–1 Hz bandwidth; permits more complex LSTM/transformer inference

### 5.2 Representative Control Demonstrations

**Melt-pool size control (LPBF)**: Several groups (Renken et al., Shkoruta et al., Grasso et al.) have demonstrated PID controllers that maintain constant melt-pool area by modulating laser power in real time based on co-axial camera measurements. ML contributes to the sensing (melt-pool segmentation) rather than the controller logic in these systems.

**Layer height control (DED)**: In DED, standoff distance and layer height vary with geometry. ML-based controllers using structured-light measurements have demonstrated closed-loop layer height control at 1 Hz bandwidth on simple geometries.

**Thermal gradient minimization (DED/WAAM)**: RL agents trained in FEA simulations and then fine-tuned on hardware have demonstrated 20–40% reduction in residual stress magnitude in simple beam geometries compared to fixed parameter strategies. Generalization to complex geometries remains limited.

## 6. Simulation-Based vs. Hardware-Validated Approaches: The Gap

This section addresses the central tension in the field — the large discrepancy between what is demonstrated in simulation studies and what is achieved on physical hardware.

### 6.1 Why Simulation Studies Dominate

Generating labeled training data for ML models requires knowing the ground truth: actual porosity locations, residual stress fields, microstructure maps. In hardware studies, obtaining this ground truth requires destructive inspection (sectioning + SEM/EBSD, CT scanning) that is expensive and time-consuming. FEA thermal simulations can generate unlimited labeled data cheaply.

As a result, the majority of published ML models for AM process control are trained on:
- FEA-generated thermal histories (Abaqus, ANSYS, Autodesk Netfabb, custom codes)
- CFD-generated melt-pool dynamics (OpenFOAM, Flow-3D)
- Physics-based surrogate models

These simulation-trained models are then evaluated on held-out simulation data, producing performance metrics (R², RMSE, classification accuracy) that can be misleadingly optimistic.

### 6.2 Specific Gap Categories

#### 6.2.1 Sensor Noise and Calibration Drift

FEA simulations provide clean temperature fields. Physical IR cameras suffer from:
- Emissivity uncertainty (varies with surface texture, oxidation state, temperature)
- Lens aberrations and vignetting
- Background radiation from the heated build chamber
- Temporal drift in detector sensitivity across long builds

ML models trained on clean simulation data routinely fail on hardware because their input distribution has shifted. A model that learned to identify keyhole formation from smooth temperature gradients may not recognize the same event masked by emissivity noise.

**Current mitigations**: Domain randomization (adding simulated noise to training data), domain adaptation (adversarial training to align simulation and hardware feature distributions), and online calibration routines.

#### 6.2.2 Model Fidelity vs. Computational Cost Trade-offs

High-fidelity FEA simulations that resolve melt-pool dynamics at micron scales and microsecond time steps are computationally prohibitive for full-part builds. ML training datasets therefore typically use one of:
- **High-fidelity short-domain simulations** (single track or small region): Does not capture part-level geometry effects
- **Low-fidelity full-part simulations** (inherent strain or reduced-order thermal models): Misses local melt-pool physics

Neither captures the full range of phenomena that appear in hardware builds.

#### 6.2.3 Geometry Generalization

Simulation studies often train and test on simple canonical geometries (cubes, cylinders, thin walls). Hardware builds for aerospace, biomedical, or tooling applications involve complex overhangs, lattice structures, and variable cross-sections. ML models that perform well on training geometries exhibit significant performance degradation on out-of-distribution geometries in hardware tests — a well-documented but poorly quantified problem.

#### 6.2.4 Alloy-Specific Phenomena Not Captured in Simulation

Many alloys used in metal AM (IN718, Ti-6Al-4V, 316L stainless steel, AlSi10Mg) exhibit complex behavior that is challenging to model accurately:
- Solid-state phase transformations with strong path dependence
- Precipitation reactions at intermediate temperatures
- Anisotropic thermal conductivity that evolves with texture development

FEA thermal models often use temperature-averaged material properties and ignore these effects. ML models trained on such simulations inherit these approximations. When deployed on hardware, discrepancies in material response lead to systematic prediction errors.

#### 6.2.5 Build Chamber Atmosphere and Powder Variability

Real builds are affected by:
- Spattering that redeposits on powder beds, changing local absorptivity
- Oxygen contamination of powder causing surface oxidation films
- Particle size distribution variation across powder lots

None of these are typically included in simulation training data, but all affect sensor readings and material response in hardware builds.

#### 6.2.6 Latency and Real-Time Constraints

Simulation studies evaluate ML models offline on pre-recorded data. Real-time deployment requires inference within the control loop latency budget. For LPBF at scan speeds of 1 m/s and voxel sizes of 50 µm, the time budget per voxel is 50 µs — far below the inference time of any current deep learning model. Even layer-level control at 10–30 s/layer is challenging for transformer-based models without careful optimization.

Hardware implementations typically trade model complexity for inference speed, deploying simpler (and less accurate) models than those evaluated in simulation studies.

### 6.3 Hardware Validation Studies: What Has Been Demonstrated

Despite the gap, there are genuinely hardware-validated demonstrations worth noting:

- **CNN-based melt-pool monitoring (LPBF)**: Implemented in commercial systems (EOS, Trumpf, SLM Solutions, GE Additive). These monitor melt-pool intensity and geometry but provide alerts rather than closed-loop correction. Latency is handled by processing on dedicated embedded processors.

- **LSTM-based anomaly detection in DED**: Haley et al. (Sandia National Laboratories) demonstrated LSTM anomaly detection on physical DED hardware, showing detection of deliberate process perturbations (powder feed interruptions) with 85% recall. Training used a combination of simulation and hardware data.

- **Bayesian optimization of process parameters**: Demonstrated in hardware across multiple groups; typically requires 20–100 physical builds to converge to near-optimal parameters. Not real-time control but validated in-the-loop optimization.

- **RL-based scan strategy optimization with hardware validation**: Wang et al. (2023, MIT) demonstrated an RL agent trained in FEA simulation and fine-tuned on 12 hardware builds achieving statistically significant reduction in porosity vs. manufacturer-default parameters for IN718 LPBF.

### 6.4 Quantifying the Gap

Studies that explicitly compare simulation and hardware performance of the same ML model reveal substantial degradation:
- Classification accuracy for porosity prediction: typically drops from 90–95% (simulation) to 65–80% (hardware) without domain adaptation
- RMSE for thermal field prediction: increases by 2–5x from simulation to hardware
- RL policy reward (part quality metric): decreases by 30–60% from simulation to hardware without fine-tuning

These numbers are not universal — they depend strongly on the specific alloy, machine, geometry, and sensor type — but they indicate that simulation performance is a poor proxy for hardware performance.

## 7. Approaches to Bridging the Simulation–Hardware Gap

### 7.1 Transfer Learning and Domain Adaptation

Pre-train on large simulation datasets; fine-tune on small hardware datasets. This is the most widely used approach. Domain-adversarial neural networks (DANNs) explicitly align the feature distributions of simulation and hardware data, improving transfer. Recent results show DANNs recover 15–25% of the accuracy lost in naive transfer.

### 7.2 Digital Twins for Co-Training

A digital twin — a continuously updated simulation model calibrated to match the physical machine — can generate simulation data that is much closer to hardware reality than a generic FEA model. ML models trained on digital twin data generalize better to hardware. The bottleneck is building and maintaining accurate digital twins, which themselves require significant calibration effort.

### 7.3 Multi-Fidelity Learning

Multi-fidelity models explicitly represent the relationship between low-fidelity simulation data and high-fidelity hardware data. Gaussian process-based multi-fidelity approaches (Kennedy-O'Hagan framework) or neural network-based multi-fidelity architectures allow leveraging large simulation datasets while correcting for known fidelity gaps.

### 7.4 Physics-Constrained Data Augmentation

Augmenting hardware datasets by applying known physical transformations (e.g., using the heat equation to generate plausible thermal fields consistent with observed boundary conditions) reduces the need for large hardware datasets while maintaining physical realism.

### 7.5 Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) for Deployment

Even when the simulation-hardware gap cannot be fully closed, calibrated uncertainty estimates allow the control system to know when its predictions are unreliable. Ensemble methods, Monte Carlo dropout, and Bayesian neural networks have been applied to melt-pool monitoring to provide uncertainty-aware quality predictions. High-uncertainty predictions can trigger conservative control responses (pausing the build, reducing energy input) rather than acting on potentially erroneous predictions.

## 8. Current Gaps and Open Problems

### 8.1 Standardized Benchmark Datasets

The field lacks standardized public datasets that would allow rigorous comparison of ML architectures. Most published results use proprietary or lab-specific builds with different machines, alloys, geometries, and sensing setups. Without benchmarks, it is impossible to determine which architectural advances are genuine improvements vs. overfitting to specific setups.

**Notable exceptions**: The NIST AM-Bench challenge provides some standardized benchmarks, but coverage of ML-relevant sensing data is limited.

### 8.2 Multi-Modal Sensor Fusion

Most ML models use a single sensor modality. Combining thermal (IR), acoustic (AE), and geometric (profilometry) data should improve inference reliability and cover blind spots of individual sensors. Multi-modal fusion architectures (cross-modal attention, contrastive learning) have been explored in other domains but are underexplored in AM.

### 8.3 Sub-Surface Defect Detection Without Destructive Inspection

Current in-situ sensing only observes surface and near-surface conditions. Sub-surface porosity and cracks are invisible until CT scanning or sectioning. ML methods that infer sub-surface state from surface observations (essentially an inverse problem) are an active research area but lack validated hardware demonstrations.

### 8.4 Long-Build Stability and Continual Learning

Sensor statistics drift over long builds (hours to days for large WAAM components) due to thermal transients in the build chamber, powder depletion, and component growth. ML models trained offline do not adapt to this drift. Continual learning approaches that update model parameters online without catastrophic forgetting are needed but have not been demonstrated in physical AM systems.

### 8.5 Multi-Material and Gradient Alloy Systems

Metal AM increasingly targets functionally graded materials and multi-material builds. ML models trained on single-alloy data do not generalize to these systems. Few-shot or zero-shot adaptation methods are needed.

### 8.6 Interpretability and Regulatory Acceptance

For safety-critical applications (aerospace, medical implants), ML-based quality certification requires interpretable models and defensible uncertainty bounds. Current black-box deep learning approaches are difficult to certify under AS9100 or ISO 13485 frameworks. Explainability methods (SHAP, attention visualization) are being applied to AM monitoring, but interpretability remains a barrier to industrial deployment.

### 8.7 End-to-End Process Parameter to Property Prediction

A complete model would map process parameters → thermal history → microstructure → mechanical properties in a single differentiable pipeline, enabling gradient-based optimization from property targets to process parameters. While individual links in this chain have been modeled with ML, end-to-end hardware-validated demonstrations are absent.

| Architecture | Primary Role in AM | Temporal Handling | Simulation-Only? | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CNN | Melt-pool segmentation, defect classification from images | None (frame-wise) | No — hardware deployed | No temporal context |
| LSTM / GRU | Thermal history modeling, anomaly detection, residual stress prediction | Explicit sequential state | Mostly simulation | Long-sequence vanishing gradient |
| TCN | Thermal time-series classification | Dilated causal convolutions | Mostly simulation | Fixed receptive field |

## 10. Conclusions

Machine learning has become integral to realizing the quality control promise of metal additive manufacturing. The field has progressed from simple CNN-based melt-pool monitoring to sophisticated spatiotemporal transformers and RL-based control systems. The sequential and temporal nature of thermal history — the key link between process parameters and material properties — is most robustly handled by LSTM/GRU architectures in current deployments, with transformer-based models emerging as a research frontier that offers superior long-range context modeling at higher computational cost.

The dominant unresolved challenge is the simulation-to-hardware gap. The vast majority of ML models with strong published performance metrics are validated only in simulation, and substantial performance degradation occurs upon deployment on physical hardware due to sensor noise, emissivity uncertainty, geometry generalization failures, and real-time latency constraints. Closing this gap requires:

1. Standardized public benchmark datasets with matched simulation and hardware data
2. Digital twin co-training and multi-fidelity learning architectures
3. Domain adaptation methods specific to AM sensor modalities
4. Continual learning for long-build drift
5. Lightweight model architectures that meet real-time inference budgets on embedded hardware

Progress on these fronts will determine whether ML-based process control transitions from laboratory demonstrations to industrial certification — a transition that is commercially critical but technically incomplete as of 2026.
